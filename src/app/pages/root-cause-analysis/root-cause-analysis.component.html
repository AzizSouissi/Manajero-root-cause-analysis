Root Cause Analysis (Ishikawa)



A diagram of fishbone or an Ishikawa diagram helps us to identify the root causes of a problem or risk and develop solutions to those problems. It is shaped like a fish skeleton, with the problem statement at the head and the causes branching off like bones. Fishbone diagrams can be a helpful tool for teams to brainstorm and identify the root causes of a problem. They can also be used to track progress over time and ensure that all possible causes of a problem have been considered. In this article, we will study a fishbone diagram in detail, its components, and the steps to create a fishbone diagram.



First step

Why (Purpose)


Root Cause Analysis (RCA) using the Ishikawa (Fishbone) diagram is a technique used to identify the underlying causes of a problem. The purpose is to systematically break down potential causes of an issue to pinpoint the root cause, thereby enabling effective solutions and preventing recurrence.

Second step

How (Implementation Process)


1. Identify the Problem: Clearly define the problem or effect you are investigating.

2. Create the Fishbone Diagram: Draw a large arrow pointing to the right; this represents the main problem. Draw lines branching off the main arrow for each category of potential causes.

3. Determine Major Categories: Common categories include People, Processes, Equipment, Materials, Environment, and Management. These are the main bones of the fish.

4. Brainstorm Causes: For each category, brainstorm potential causes and add them as smaller branches off each main category line.

5. Analyze and Identify Root Causes: Review the diagram, analyze the relationships between causes, and identify the root causes contributing most significantly to the problem.

6. Prioritize and Address Root Causes: Develop action plans to address the identified root causes.

Tools and Techniques


Brainstorming Sessions: Gather a team to brainstorm potential causes for each category.

5 Whys Technique: Ask "Why?" repeatedly until you reach the fundamental cause of the problem.

Data Collection and Analysis: Use data to verify and validate the causes identified during brainstorming.

Pareto Analysis: Focus on the causes that have the most significant impact, often illustrated by the Pareto principle (80/20 rule).

Fourth step

What If (Testing and Validation)


Hypothesis Testing: Implement temporary solutions based on identified root causes and observe the results.

Pilot Projects: Test solutions on a small scale before full implementation.

Monitoring and Measurement: Continuously monitor the outcomes to ensure the problem is resolved.

Feedback Loops: Gather feedback from stakeholders to validate the effectiveness of the solutions.

Fifth step

Continuous Improvement


Regular Reviews: Conduct periodic reviews of processes and outcomes to identify new issues or improvements.

Training and Development: Educate employees on root cause analysis and problem-solving techniques.

Standardization: Incorporate successful solutions into standard operating procedures.

Continuous Learning: Stay updated on best practices and new techniques in root cause analysis and quality management.



Example of an Ishikawa diagram




The Fishbone diagram example provided illustrates a structured approach to analyzing cause and effect relationships within a production process. It visually organizes potential causes into categories that branch out from a central problem statement, resembling the skeletal structure of a fish. This diagram format enables teams to systematically explore various factors contributing to issues or inefficiencies in production. By categorizing causes such as equipment, materials, methods, people, and environment, the diagram highlights interdependencies and helps prioritize areas for improvement. Its clear and organized layout aids in fostering team collaboration, brainstorming, and developing targeted solutions to enhance process efficiency and quality control. The diagram's simplicity yet comprehensive coverage of potential factors makes it a valuable tool for continuous improvement initiatives in industrial settings.
